# Taiwan's Elections and Party Politics

From the "democratic showcase" elections of the 1950s to the first direct presidential election in 1996, Taiwan spent nearly half a century achieving a peaceful transition from authoritarianism to democracy. This process was filled with resistance and compromise, idealism and pragmatism, ultimately creating today's vibrant and diverse democratic Taiwan.

## The Martial Law Era: Limited Democratic Experiments

### "Free China's" Democratic Showcase

In the 1950s, facing Cold War pressures, the Kuomintang (KMT) government needed to prove to the United States that it was "Free China," different from Communist China. Under strict limitations, local elections began as a democratic "showcase."

The 1950 Taiwan Provincial Assembly election marked Taiwan's first post-war democratic election. However, this "democracy" came with many restrictions: only local councilors could be elected, not governors; only representatives, not political parties; policies could be criticized but not leadership. The KMT tightly controlled the electoral process, ensuring no voices would challenge the regime.

Even so, these elections planted seeds for future democratization. Some local elites entered the political system through elections and began learning democratic governance. Most importantly, people became accustomed to expressing opinions through ballots, and democratic consciousness gradually awakened.

### Gradual Progress in Local Autonomy

Beginning in the 1960s, local elections gradually expanded. Positions like county mayors, provincial assembly members, and Taipei mayor became elective. Although still under strict KMT control, electoral competition intensified.

This period saw the emergence of important local political figures. Mayors like Yang Jinhu of Kaohsiung, Lin Chengqiu of Taichung, and Ye Tingkui of Tainan, though KMT members, had distinctive Taiwanese characteristics. They displayed different governance styles that laid the foundation for future localization politics.

More importantly, some non-KMT candidates began emerging in elections. Unable to form parties, they ran as independents with different platforms. These "dangwai" (outside the party) figures became important forces in the future democracy movement.

## The Dangwai Movement: Democracy's Herald

### The 1970s: An Era of Political Awakening

The 1970s marked a critical period of political awakening in Taiwan. Diplomatic setbacks like withdrawal from the UN and severance of ties with the US led Taiwanese society to question KMT rule. Rapid economic growth also created a new middle class demanding greater political participation.

Guo Yuxin's high vote count in a 1975 legislative supplementary election shocked the political establishment. Though he lost, his over 60% vote share revealed public dissatisfaction with KMT one-party rule. This election is considered the starting point of the dangwai movement.

Dangwai figures began challenging speech taboos. They criticized martial law, questioned the "ten-thousand-year parliament" (萬年國會), and demanded lifting the party ban in campaign speeches. Though often facing judicial persecution, democratic ideals gradually gained popular support.

### Formosa Magazine: Democracy's Torch

The founding of Formosa Magazine (美麗島雜誌) in 1979 became an important platform for the dangwai movement. The magazine brought together key dangwai figures like Kang Ningxiang, Huang Xingjie, Shi Mingde, and Xu Xinliang, forming the core group that would later become the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP).

Formosa Magazine was not just a publication but a proto-political organization. It established service centers nationwide, organized reader meetings, and functionally operated like a political party. The magazine boldly criticized the government and proposed democratic reforms, rapidly growing its circulation.

On December 10, 1979, Formosa Magazine held a human rights commemoration event in Kaohsiung, which erupted into the "Kaohsiung Incident" (美麗島事件). Military police suppressed crowds and arrested dangwai leaders. While this devastated the dangwai movement, it also showed the international community Taiwan's democratic aspirations.

### From Underground to Above Ground: The Emergence of Party Politics

After the Kaohsiung Incident, the dangwai movement didn't disappear but became more vigorous. Family members of arrested leaders entered politics, leading to the rise of "Kaohsiung Generation" female politicians like Annette Lu, Chen Ju, and Zhou Qingyu.

In the early 1980s, dangwai figures used names like "Dangwai Public Policy Association" and "Dangwai Editorial Association" to conduct de facto party organizing activities. They supported each other in elections and formed common platforms, already possessing party characteristics.

In 1985, dangwai figures formally announced the establishment of the "Dangwai Research Association," the DPP's predecessor. Though the government didn't recognize its legality, it operated de facto and prepared for formal party establishment.

## Lifting Martial Law and Democratization: Critical Turning Points

### 1986: The First Year of Party Politics

1986 marked a watershed in Taiwan's party politics. Under Chiang Ching-kuo's "political reform" policy, the political atmosphere gradually loosened. Dangwai figures seized the opportunity, formally establishing the Democratic Progressive Party on September 28, breaking the KMT's one-party dominance.

Though the DPP's establishment violated existing laws, the government chose tolerance. This decision held great significance, representing the KMT's willingness to coexist with opposition parties, opening the door to democratic transition.

The DPP's first major election was the late-1986 legislative election. Despite being organized for only two months, the DPP won 22.2% of votes and 12 legislative seats. This exceeded all expectations, proving Taiwanese society's desire for democracy.

### The Shock of Lifting Martial Law: A New Era of Free Speech

On July 15, 1987, Taiwan formally lifted martial law. This historic moment not only ended military rule but opened a new era of free speech. Political taboos were broken one by one, media began reporting freely, and people could openly discuss politics.

Post-martial law elections became more intense. Candidates could criticize the president, question government policies, and even propose unification or independence positions. Campaign debates became public focal points, with television broadcasts allowing all Taiwanese to participate in the democratic process.

This period also saw many new political figures emerge. They no longer needed to hide political positions and could openly compete for votes. Taiwan's political scene became more diverse and competitive.

## Party Alternation: Democracy's Maturation

### 1996: The Milestone of Direct Presidential Election

On March 23, 1996, Taiwan held its first direct presidential election. This was the first in Republic of China history and the first direct presidential election in Chinese societies. All 21 million Taiwanese had voting rights, with a 76% turnout rate.

This election's significance transcended politics. It symbolized Taiwan's democratic maturity and demonstrated to the world the democratic capacity of Chinese societies. Lee Teng-hui won with 54% of votes, but more importantly, the entire election process was peaceful and orderly without unrest.

The PRC launched missiles to threaten Taiwan during the election, inadvertently uniting Taiwanese society. Though parties had different platforms, all supported democratic elections. This "democratic baptism" strengthened Taiwan's democratic system.

### 2000: The First Party Alternation

In the 2000 presidential election, DPP candidate Chen Shui-bian won, ending 55 years of KMT rule. This was Taiwan's first party alternation, of epochal significance.

More importantly, the power transfer was completely peaceful. Though the KMT lost power, it didn't resist but chose to respect public opinion. This "gentlemanly competition" spirit earned Taiwan's democracy international praise.

Chen Shui-bian's inauguration promise of "Four Nos and One Without" (四不一沒有) showed the DPP's pragmatic attitude after taking power. The new government didn't pursue political revenge but worked toward national unity. This moderate transition was an important characteristic of Taiwan's democracy.

### 2008 and 2016: The Normalization of Alternation

Ma Ying-jeou's 2008 presidential victory returned the KMT to power, completing the second party alternation. Tsai Ing-wen's 2016 victory brought the DPP back to power, achieving the third party alternation.

These alternations all followed the same pattern: intense but fair electoral competition, results accepted by all parties, and peaceful power transfers. Party alternation evolved from "historical event" to "normal phenomenon," marking mature democracy.

Each alternation brought policy adjustments while maintaining stable institutional frameworks. Regardless of which party governed, all had to follow constitutional order, accept media supervision, and face voter scrutiny. This "institutions over individuals" democratic culture is Taiwan's most precious asset.

## The Formation of Electoral Culture

### Campaign Rallies: Taiwan's Unique Democratic Carnivals

Taiwan's elections are most characterized by lively campaign rallies. Candidates rent large venues, invite performers, distribute gifts, and attract crowds. Campaign rallies aren't just political activities but community carnivals.

This electoral culture reflects Taiwanese social characteristics: valuing personal relationships, enjoying excitement, and caring about face. Candidates must be "down to earth" and build emotional connections with voters. Pure policy debates aren't enough—they must also sing, act, and perform.

Campaign rallies also created new political language. Terms like "dong-suan" (當選, "get elected"), "down-shelf" (下架), and "liberation" (光復) became characteristic Taiwanese political vocabulary. These vivid and creative terms display Taiwanese humor.

### Television Debates: Platforms for Rational Discussion

As democracy deepened, television debates became important electoral elements. Candidates must articulate platforms, face challenges, and demonstrate abilities before cameras. Good debate performance can reverse electoral fortunes, while poor performance may end political careers.

Television debates elevated elections' rational components. Voters not only consider candidates' charisma but also policy content. This change reflects Taiwanese voters' maturity and promotes politicians' professionalization.

The internet era further transformed electoral ecology. Candidates directly engage voters through social media, and young voters become key forces. Traditional campaign rallies remain important, but digital marketing cannot be ignored.

## Democratic Challenges and Reflections

### The Shadow of Money Politics

Though Taiwan's democracy succeeded, it faces many challenges. Money politics is among the most serious problems. Huge campaign costs easily create benefit transfers. Some enterprises influence policy through political donations, distorting democracy's spirit.

Though vote-buying has improved compared to the past, it hasn't been completely eliminated. Particularly in local elections, some candidates still seek votes through red envelopes and gifts. This behavior not only violates law but harms democratic quality.

The government attempts to regulate political donations through legislation and establish public election financing to reduce money's influence on politics. But truly solving the problem requires collective efforts from all sectors of society.

### Media Chaos and Disinformation

Media freedom is democracy's cornerstone, but Taiwan's media also faces problems. Some media outlets have biased positions and spread disinformation, misleading voters. Especially in the social media era, fake news spreads extremely fast, affecting electoral fairness.

The government attempts to regulate disinformation through legislation but faces questions about free speech. Balancing combating fake news with protecting free speech is a common challenge for democratic societies.

Media literacy education becomes an important issue. Voters must learn to identify disinformation and think rationally about political issues. This isn't just the government's responsibility but every citizen's duty.

### Generational Differences and Political Participation

Younger generations have different political attitudes than older ones. They focus more on issues and don't blindly support specific parties. This change helps rationalize politics but also brings new challenges.

How to attract young people to participate in politics? How to make their voices heard? These are questions politicians must consider. Internet politics, youth political participation, and issue-oriented political culture are changing Taiwan's democratic landscape.

## Taiwan Democracy's Global Significance

Taiwan's democratic transition is viewed internationally as a successful model. Without external intervention, through peaceful resistance and rational dialogue, it completed the transition from authoritarianism to democracy. This experience provides important inspiration for other developing countries.

More importantly, Taiwan proved that Chinese societies can practice democratic politics. This achievement shattered the "Asian values" myth, proving democracy is a universal value not limited by culture.

Though Taiwan's democracy continues evolving and faces various challenges, its basic framework is solid. Electoral systems are fair and transparent, party alternation is normalized, and civil society is thriving. These are Taiwan's democracy's precious assets.

Reviewing Taiwan's democratization process, we see a society's collective effort. From dangwai movement pioneers to enlightened politicians supporting democracy, from media willing to monitor to voters casting rational ballots, everyone contributed.

Taiwan democracy's story continues being written. Facing new challenges, we need to continuously improve institutions and enhance democratic quality. But one thing is certain: democracy has deeply rooted in Taiwanese society, becoming our most precious common asset.
